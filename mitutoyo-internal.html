Talk Track
How to Navigate This Conversation
You are at Stage 02. The goal of the next conversation is to understand the problem, not to explain the product. These questions are sequenced to build context before you say anything about capability.
1
Open with what you know, not what you're selling.
"I know there is an integration project in motion and I want to make sure I understand the actual problem before I say anything about what we do. Can you tell me what you're trying to connect and where the current approach breaks down?" This signals that you have done homework and you are not there to run a generic discovery script.
2
Map the systems they are trying to connect.
Mitutoyo America runs a service business (calibration and repair), a direct sales business, and distributes through channel partners. The integration pain could be in any of these: service data to CRM, order management to ERP, customer portal to fulfillment, or instrument data to customer QMS. Ask: "Which systems are you trying to get talking to each other right now?" Map the answer before you scope anything.
3
Understand what they tried before and why it didn't work.
A MuleSoft deal was in the system as "Dead - No Opportunity" in 2022 at $125K. Something killed it before it started. Ask Dan Rogers: "Has your team looked at integration projects before? What happened?" The answer tells you whether the prior failure was budget, internal priority, technical complexity, or something else. Don't assume you know.
4
Do not mention the commercial deals that just died unless they bring it up.
The $640K Agentforce + Sales EE deal and the $91.8K Marketing + AF deal both died in June 2026 — Dead-Lost. These were likely separate conversations with Allen Cius on the commercial side. Dan Rogers and Mike Thomas on the IT side may or may not have been involved. Do not reference them. If they come up, acknowledge the history honestly: "I know we have had some big conversations that didn't move forward. I'm not here to revisit those. I want to focus on what you are actually trying to solve right now."
5
Close on a defined next step, not a general follow-up.
Stage 02 should exit into a clear problem statement and a scoping session. Don't leave the call with "I'll send some materials." Leave with: "Let me put together a scoping document based on what you've told me. Can we get 45 minutes on the calendar in the next two weeks to walk through it?" Specificity builds credibility that generic follow-up destroys.

Responses to the Likely Pushback

That is exactly why I want to take a different approach here. Every prior conversation was about large platform deployments. This one is about a specific integration problem. If we can solve that problem and you see real value, then maybe a bigger conversation makes sense eventually. But I'm not asking you to trust a platform right now. I'm asking you to let me understand one problem.

If they already use it: "Then we're talking about making it work better, not convincing you to adopt something new. Tell me what you have and where it's falling short." If they looked and passed: "What stopped it? I want to understand what was missing, because the product has changed and the scoping approach has changed. If the same problem is still there, it's worth a fresh look."
Our IT resources are stretched thin right now.
That is the exact reason to scope the integration correctly before building anything. A poorly scoped integration project consumes IT time indefinitely. A well-scoped one delivers a defined outcome in a defined window. What would it take to get to a point where you could evaluate whether this is worth the resource investment?
We're a subsidiary. Big technology decisions go through Japan.
That is worth understanding — at what dollar threshold does a technology investment require parent company approval? If we can scope this to a Level 1 engagement that stays within your authority, can you move on it? And if it does need parent approval, who would you bring in from the Japan side, and what do they need to see to say yes?
