Account Context
Why the standard CRM pitch has failed here and what the right angle looks like
Who they are

McElroy Metal makes metal roofing panels and wall panels. Make-to-order. They sell through a dealer network and direct to roofing contractors. $362M revenue, 750 employees, headquartered in Bossier City, LA.

Their business lives in the spec decision. A contractor building a bid chooses a panel profile, gauge, coating, and color. That decision drives the order. Right now, most of that decision-making happens over the phone or by calling a dealer.

The MuleSoft failure signal

They bought MuleSoft Order Automation in January 2024. The implementation had low utilization and they churned eight months later. They told the rep they'd renew. They did not.

This is not a "we don't care about digital" signal. It's a "we tried to solve this problem and the solution did not fit the people who needed to use it." The backend integration did not change anything the contractor or dealer experienced.

The pattern in the history

Sales Cloud, Service Cloud, Slack, and Manufacturing Cloud have all gone No Decision or Lost. The internal CRM pitch has been tried multiple times across multiple people since 2013. It has not landed.

The $300K Manufacturing Cloud Big Bet in 2021 went No Decision. They evaluated it seriously. Four years later the business has grown and the platform is more mature. Worth revisiting but do not lead with it.

The fresh angle

The pitch that has not been tried is customer-facing: a digital configurator that lets contractors spec panels, get pricing, and confirm lead times without calling anyone. That solves a contractor problem, not an internal workflow problem.

Adoption is built in because the user is the contractor who wants to find McElroy's product. Compare that to MuleSoft, where adoption required internal team behavior change. This is a fundamentally different deployment dynamic.

Talk Track
Calibrated for a re-engagement conversation with an account that has a MuleSoft history
Opener

"McElroy made a real investment in order automation a couple of years ago. I'd like to understand what you learned from that experience, and whether a customer-facing configurator for contractors and dealers is a conversation that's on your radar."

Let them answer before you say anything else. The answer to that question tells you everything you need to know about how to frame the next 30 minutes.

Discovery Questions
  • What was the primary reason MuleSoft adoption was low — was it the dealers, the contractors, or the internal workflow?
  • How do roofing contractors spec McElroy panels today before calling a dealer?
  • What percentage of your dealer quote requests come in after business hours?
  • How do you track committed volumes from your top dealers against production capacity?
The Value Pivot

"The MuleSoft order automation solved a plumbing problem — connecting systems. The configurator solves a customer problem — giving contractors a way to spec and order at 9pm. Those are different things."

"One creates internal efficiency. The other captures demand you're currently missing. And the adoption dynamic is completely different because the user is the contractor who wants to find your product."

The Ask

A 30-minute demo of what a building materials configurator looks like for a make-to-order manufacturer with complex product options.

"I want to show you a comparable manufacturer who built this for their dealer and contractor channel. After that, you'll know whether it's a conversation worth having internally. That's all I'm asking for today."

Objection Handling
Five responses for the five things this account will say
We already tried Salesforce (MuleSoft) and it didn't work out.
You're right, and I appreciate you being direct about that. The MuleSoft implementation was trying to solve a backend order routing problem. This is a customer-facing configurator that gives contractors a way to spec your panels and get lead times before they call anyone. Different problem, different solution, different adoption curve — because the person using the tool is the contractor who wants to find your product, not an internal team being asked to change their workflow.
Our dealers handle the contractor relationships.
They do, and this doesn't change that. The configurator serves contractors at the moment they're building a bid — at 9pm when the dealer is closed. When the contractor calls the dealer the next morning, they already have a spec and a preliminary quote. The dealer's job gets easier, not harder.
We evaluated Manufacturing Cloud in 2021 and didn't move forward.
A lot has changed since then. The platform is significantly more mature, the implementation cost is lower, and the use cases for a make-to-order manufacturer — production run visibility, dealer volume tracking, lead time accuracy — are better proven. I'd rather show you what's different now than try to relitigate 2021.
IT doesn't have bandwidth for another implementation.
The configurator is a hosted SaaS product, not an on-premise build. The implementation is configuration, not development. And critically, Cliff Barnes has the business systems context from the MuleSoft work that makes him the right person to scope what's actually involved. I'd like to get his read on it.
We're not making any platform decisions right now.
Understood. Can I ask what the threshold is for getting something on the roadmap? Because the contractor spec problem — bids being built after hours without a digital tool — is a revenue leak that compounds every quarter. I'm not asking for a decision today. I'm asking for 30 minutes to show you what the problem looks like solved.
