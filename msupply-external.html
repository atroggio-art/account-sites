Strategic Brief — Commerce & Data

Your Customer Buys
Three Ways.
You See None of Them Together.

mSupply distributes appliance, HVAC, and plumbing parts across 40+ retail branches, a growing ecommerce channel, and a network of field reps. Three channels. Three datasets. No unified customer view. And an acquisition pipeline that adds a new silo every quarter.

$963M Annual Revenue
40+ US Locations
3 Sales Channels
The Problem

Every Acquisition Makes the Problem Bigger

A technician walks into a Marcone branch in Florida on Monday, orders the same part online on Wednesday, and calls a rep about a bulk purchase on Friday. Those are three separate records in three separate systems. No one sees the full relationship.

That invisibility has a cost: reps can't spot churn signals. Branch staff can't see online order history. Marketing can't segment by actual purchase behavior. And when you acquire a new brand, you add another island instead of adding to a unified customer picture.

The parts business is won on speed, availability, and relationship. Right now the relationship data doesn't exist in a usable form.

Channel Blindness

A technician's full purchase history is split across a POS, an ecommerce platform, and a CRM. No single view exists to drive pricing, service, or retention decisions.

Acquisition Sprawl

Each acquired brand brings its own systems. Without a unified data layer, the portfolio grows but the customer intelligence doesn't compound.

Order Management Gaps

Fulfillment across 40+ locations with no centralized order orchestration means inconsistent lead times, manual escalations, and missed cross-branch inventory.

What Changes

A Connected Operating Model

Three capabilities that eliminate the channel and data gaps holding back growth.

01
Unified Customer Identity
Merge retail, ecommerce, and rep-sold transactions into a single technician profile that every team member can see and act on.
  • Resolved customer record across all channels
  • Full transaction history in one view
  • Acquisition brands connected on intake
  • Real-time sync, no batch reconciliation
02
Omnichannel Order Management
Centralized order orchestration across all 40+ branches so fulfillment is visible, consistent, and optimized regardless of how the order was placed.
  • Single order queue across all channels
  • Cross-branch inventory visibility
  • Automated routing by availability and proximity
  • Returns and exceptions handled in one system
03
Self-Service Technician Portal
A branded B2B ordering experience where techs can browse the full catalog, manage accounts, check order status, and set up recurring orders without calling a rep.
  • Account-based pricing and catalogs
  • Real-time order and delivery tracking
  • Invoice history and account management
  • Connects to unified customer profile
Why Now

Three Forces That Make This the Right Quarter

01
The Acquisition Window
The PE mandate is to acquire and integrate. Every acquisition completed before a unified data platform exists adds cost and complexity that compounds. The earlier the foundation is built, the cleaner each subsequent integration becomes.
02
Ecommerce Is Accelerating
As online parts ordering grows, the gap between channel data widens. Building the unified customer model now -- while ecommerce is scaling but not yet dominant -- is far less expensive than retrofitting it when every channel is firing at full volume.
03
Tech Buyer Expectations Have Shifted
Independent technicians now expect consumer-grade digital experiences from their suppliers. Distributors who build self-service ordering and account visibility first capture loyalty that is very hard to reverse. Those who wait hand that loyalty to competitors.
