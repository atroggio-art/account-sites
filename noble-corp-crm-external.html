The Basics
What a CRM Actually Is
A CRM is a single platform where your company tracks every interaction it has with customers and prospects. Think of it as the shared memory of your commercial organization.

Who you have met. What was discussed. Where a contract stands. What needs to happen next. All of it in one place, visible to anyone who needs it, searchable, and permanently attached to the account record rather than the person who was in the room.

That is the core of it. Everything else, forecasting, pipeline management, AI agents, automation, builds on top of that foundation. But the foundation is simply this: customer knowledge that belongs to the company, not to individuals.

Without it, critical context disappears when people do.

When someone leaves, changes roles, or just gets pulled into another priority, everything they knew about their accounts goes with them. Noble's customer relationships are too valuable and too hard-won to live in personal inboxes.

01
Without it, there is no way to see the whole picture.

Leadership cannot see what is in progress, what is stalling, or where the team's attention is actually going. You are managing by asking around, not by looking at data. That is a forecasting problem, a prioritization problem, and a competitive problem.

02
Without it, every bid starts from scratch.

Prior assessments, cost drivers, risk factors, scope assumptions from similar work — all of that sits in spreadsheets and people's memories. Your team rebuilds it every time instead of building on what already exists.

Customer Example
A Global Jack-Up Driller That Chose to Fix This.
A global offshore jack-up drilling contractor managing a large fleet with field teams in remote locations. The problems they came in with will sound familiar.
JU
Global Jack-Up Drilling Contractor Large fleet · Remote field teams · Multi-region operations
The Problems They Had
  • Rig-based crews had no real-time visibility back to onshore operations. Activity was reconstructed after the fact, not captured live.
  • Supplier and procurement relationships managed through email. No shared record of what was agreed, promised, or pending.
  • Asset maintenance and inspection data siloed across the fleet. No consistent way to see risk across rigs.
  • Commercial and operations teams working in separate systems. The handoff between what was sold and what was delivered broke down repeatedly.
What Salesforce Made Possible
  • Field teams log activity in real time, mobile-first and offline-capable, so nothing is lost between the rig and the office.
  • Procurement, planning, and maintenance teams share one live view of assets and open work. No more chasing updates across inboxes.
  • Commercial context travels with every engagement. Operations teams inherit full background, not a summary email.
  • Leadership has a fleet-wide view of what is happening, what is at risk, and where to focus, without asking around to get it.
The result: less time chasing information, faster decisions, and a commercial organization that operates as one team. They chose Salesforce because the alternative, leaving critical knowledge in spreadsheets and inboxes, was costing them deals, efficiency, and competitive ground. Noble Corp Specifically
What This Looks Like for Noble.
Five patterns that show up in every offshore drilling commercial organization operating without a shared system. Each one has a direct fix.
01
No standard way of working across the commercial team

When every rep manages their own spreadsheet and inbox, there is no corporate process and no way to forecast, prioritize, or scale. Salesforce gives everyone a shared system so leadership has a real-time view of what is in progress, what is stalling, and what needs attention. The visibility does not come from asking around. It comes from looking at the data.

02
Customer knowledge trapped in people's heads

When a contact tours a facility, those conversations, who attended, what was discussed, what concerns were raised, should belong to Noble Corp, not just to the person who was in the room. Salesforce captures and stores that activity automatically so it is searchable, transferable, and never lost when someone moves on or gets pulled in a different direction.

03
Bidding everything equally

Not all tenders deserve the same effort. Salesforce surfaces which opportunities are most strategic, most likely to win, and where your technical resources will have the highest impact so your team focuses on the must-wins, not just the most recent request that landed in someone's inbox.

04
Repeated work on similar assets

When the same asset class comes up for a new customer bid, teams should not be starting from scratch. Salesforce stores prior assessments, cost estimates, and risk data so they can be retrieved and reused, building on what is already done rather than rebuilding it. Every bid gets smarter than the last.

05
The bid-to-execution gap

The context that lives inside a bid, scope assumptions, risk factors, cost drivers, almost never makes it to the operations team intact. Salesforce keeps all of that attached to the opportunity so operations inherits full context, not a summary email. Over time, execution data feeds back into future estimates, making them more accurate with every contract.
