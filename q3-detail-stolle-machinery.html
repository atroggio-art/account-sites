The Story
Pain When Stolle Machinery's global customers need parts or order visibility, they're picking up a phone instead of logging into a portal — a manual, friction-filled experience for a world-class equipment manufacturer.
Consequence Every manual interaction is a service cost, a satisfaction risk, and a missed opportunity to build the kind of self-serve loyalty that keeps customers on Stolle's platform instead of exploring alternatives.

What They Do

Stolle Machinery is the world's leading supplier of two-piece aluminum and steel can and end-making equipment. Their machines are installed in beverage, food, and consumer goods factories on every continent — making them the de facto standard for high-volume can manufacturing globally.
The Parts & Service Business
Behind every machine sale is a long-term parts, tooling, and service relationship. That aftermarket stream — replacement parts, consumables, technical service — is a recurring revenue engine that is currently largely phone-and-email driven. That's the commerce opportunity.
Global Installed Base
Thousands of machines in operation worldwide means a large, distributed customer base placing regular orders. A self-serve commerce channel for parts and service requests is a direct operational improvement for both Stolle and its customers.
🧭

Strategic Approach

Lead with commercial and aftermarket operations teams — these are the people who field parts requests and hear the friction firsthand.
Frame the solution as a customer experience improvement, not a technology project — "your customers can self-serve" is the pitch, not "new platform integration."
Build the business case around parts order volume and rep time spent on routine transactions — quantify the ROI before any technical conversation begins.
If IT surfaces naturally, bring them in after business alignment is secured — never as the lead audience.
