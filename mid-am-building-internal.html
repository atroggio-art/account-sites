Account Context
What you need to know before you pick up the phone
Who they are

Third-generation family business, founded 1967, Moberly MO. The Knaebel family built a regional wholesale distributor of windows, doors, siding, roofing, and cabinets serving lumber dealers across Illinois, Iowa, Kansas, and Missouri.

$235M revenue, 400 employees, six distribution centers, and four door-fabrication warehouses that build custom doors. This is a serious regional operation with nearly 60 years of dealer trust behind it.

The real opportunity

Lumber dealers across the Midwest call their Mid-Am rep to check inventory and place orders. Every one of those calls is capacity the operation absorbs manually. The door-fab side adds complexity: dealers need production timelines before committing to a job estimate, and today that means a phone call.

A dealer ordering portal with real-time inventory visibility and door-fab lead time data is the play. It serves the dealer better and recaptures rep time at the same time.

Why the prior plays went dead

Three opps went No Decision in late 2024. The December opp notes say it plainly: never got HQ leadership aligned, never built the business case. The prior play was internal CRM for reps (Sales Cloud plus Service Cloud for 40+40 employees).

Family businesses don't get excited about software for their sales reps. They get excited about things that serve their dealer customers and protect revenue. The play was wrong and it went to the wrong stakeholders.

What makes this play different

B2B Commerce (dealer portal) plus Manufacturing Cloud (door-fab run rates) is a customer-facing revenue play. That gets HQ attention at a family business in a way that internal CRM never will.

Sean Hughes is the COO, which means he IS the HQ alignment the prior opp said it needed. The opp notes said "need engagement with HQ leadership." Hughes is that person. Start there.

Talk Track
Opener, discovery, the value pivot, and the ask
The Opener

"Mid-Am has been serving lumber dealers for nearly 60 years. The question I want to ask is how those dealers place an order today, and whether there's a better way."

That is the whole frame. Do not overcomplicate the open. The simplicity of the question is the point. Let them tell you what the process looks like. You want to hear it in their words before you say anything about what you sell.

Discovery Questions

What percentage of your reps' time is reorder activity versus new dealer development?

How do dealers check inventory before committing to a project estimate?

How do you track door-fab commitments against production capacity across four warehouses?

When a dealer places a large door order, what happens on the mid-am side between that call and the delivery confirmation?

The Value Pivot

"The dealers placing the same order every two weeks do not need a rep. They need a portal. Your reps need to be spending their time on the dealer relationships that are at risk or the new dealers you have not landed yet."

"The door-fab side of this is actually the part that differentiates you. If a dealer can see lead times and production availability in real time, they stop calling to check. They commit faster because they have the information they need."

The Ask

"I would like to show you what Westlake Royal built for their dealer and contractor channel. It is the closest structural analog to what Mid-Am could build for its lumber dealer base. 30 minutes. You tell me if it is directionally right or completely off."

Objection Handling
The five objections you will hear and how to move through them
Our dealers prefer to call their rep. That is the relationship.
The portal handles routine orders. The rep handles the relationship. Those are not the same thing. A dealer who calls to place a reorder on window units they order every month does not need a relationship conversation. A dealer who is bidding a large job and needs production lead times, pricing guidance, and someone they trust is exactly who the rep should be talking to. The distributors who have built self-serve channels consistently report that reps like it more, not less, because their conversations become higher value. The rep's job gets better.
We looked at Salesforce before and it did not go anywhere.
The prior play was internal CRM for your sales reps, which is a different conversation with different stakeholders and a different business case. This is a customer-facing dealer ordering portal. The people who need to get excited about it are Sean Hughes and your dealer-facing leadership, not your sales ops team. A portal that makes it easier for your best lumber dealer accounts to place orders is a revenue and retention story, not a software adoption story. Those are genuinely different conversations.
We are a family business. We make decisions slowly and carefully.
Respect that completely. A scoped pilot with your top 20 dealer accounts does not require a company-wide decision. It requires three people to agree to a 90-day test. You pick 20 of your highest-volume dealers who you already have strong relationships with, you give them a portal, and you measure whether they use it for routine orders. If they do not, you learned something in 90 days at low cost. If they do, you have the data to make the larger decision with confidence. That is how a careful organization tests something new.
IT does not have bandwidth for a platform project right now.
Peter Trapani is your IT Project Coordinator, which means this lands on his plate. Here is what I want you to hear clearly: a hosted SaaS portal does not require IT to build and maintain infrastructure. It requires configuration, not development. Trapani's job is to configure workflows, connect to your existing ERP and inventory systems, and deploy. He is not standing up servers or writing custom code. The bandwidth question is real, but the actual lift for IT on this is much smaller than the word "platform" implies.
We do not have budget for this right now.
The ROI case is straightforward to build. If your top 50 dealer accounts shift even 30 percent of their reorders to self-serve, the rep time savings alone covers the platform cost. I can build that model using your actual numbers, your rep headcount, your average order volume, before we ever talk about a dollar figure. Let me put that together and bring it back to you. If the math does not work, I will tell you. If it does, you have something to bring into the budget conversation with a number attached to it.
