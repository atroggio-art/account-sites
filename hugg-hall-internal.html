Before I waste your time or mine, I want to understand what has gotten in the way. Is it budget approval, internal alignment, something about the product, or something else?" Name the elephant. The accounts that never close are almost always the ones where no one is willing to have that conversation.

Talk Track
How to Navigate This Account
The normal discovery-to-proposal motion will not work here. This account has been through it more than a dozen times. The only conversation worth having is the one that names the pattern and diagnoses the stopper.
1
Name the history before they do.
"I want to be upfront with you. I can see that Hugg and Hall has evaluated Salesforce many times, and none of those conversations resulted in a project. I'm not here to run the same process again. I'm here to understand what has gotten in the way, because I don't think it's indifference to the problem." Starting here signals you have done your homework and you are not going to waste their time with the same pitch.
2
Ask for the stopper, not the pain.
The standard discovery question is "what problem are you trying to solve?" That question will get you a polite answer and another No Decision. The better question for this account: "What specifically has stopped the past projects from moving forward?" Listen for: budget approval process, lack of executive champion, competing internal priorities, integration concerns, prior bad experience with implementations, or a key stakeholder who is opposed. The answer is your roadmap.
3
Understand the equipment dealer business model before the call.
Hugg & Hall sells and rents heavy equipment (likely forklifts, aerial work platforms, cranes, and construction equipment) across Arkansas and surrounding states. Their revenue model has three legs: new/used equipment sales, equipment rental, and aftermarket/service (parts, maintenance, warranty). The service and rental sides have the most repetitive operational complexity — field technicians, preventive maintenance scheduling, rental availability and utilization, contract billing. That is where the FSL and Sales Cloud plays live.
4
If they are open to continuing, scope very small.
The 2016 opp notes said the contact found a prior demo "overwhelming" and wanted to start with 15 outside rental reps. A 320-seat FSL deal is the opposite of that instinct. If there is re-engagement interest, propose a pilot: 20-30 field service technicians in one region, one use case (preventive maintenance scheduling or work order management), 90-day timeline. Make it easy to say yes. Big deals have never closed here. A small, defined pilot that delivers a result is the path to anything larger.
5
Get to the ownership level if you can.
This is a family-owned business. John Hugg is the president and his name is on the sign. In family-owned companies, technology decisions frequently require the owner's personal comfort with the investment, not just a business case that clears a financial hurdle. If you get traction at the VP level, ask directly: "Is this the kind of decision that John Hugg would want to understand personally before it moves forward?" The answer tells you whether you need an executive conversation or whether it can close at the VP level.
Objection Handling
What You Will Hear and How to Respond
We've looked at Salesforce many times. It's never been the right fit.
That is exactly what I want to understand. "Not the right fit" can mean the product wasn't right, the timing wasn't right, the price wasn't right, or the internal process for approving the investment wasn't right. Those are four different problems with four different solutions. Which one is it? I can't help you if I don't know.
We're happy with what we have right now.
What are you using for field service management and rental sales activity tracking? I'm genuinely curious, because one of two things is true: either you found something that works and you are right to stay with it, or you are living with a workaround that nobody wants to admit is a workaround. I'd like to know which.
The implementation is too complex and would take too long.
That concern has been justified before, and I'm not going to dismiss it. What would a project scope look like that felt manageable to your team? If we started with 25 field technicians in one district, one use case, 90 days — what would that require from your IT team, and is that level of commitment something that could get approved?
Budget is the issue. We can't get approval for this size of investment.
At what size does it become approvable? I'm serious — if we can scope a pilot that fits within your discretionary spend authority, we bypass the budget approval process entirely and you get a result you can use to justify the larger investment. Tell me the number and I'll build the scope around it.
We need to integrate with our rental management / DMS system first.
That is a real constraint and I appreciate you naming it. What rental management system are you running? The integration question is solvable — it is a scope and timeline question, not a technical impossibility. If I can show you that the integration is a defined, bounded piece of work, does the rest of the business case hold up?
