What Your COO Said
"Designing our operations with intention allows us to run more efficiently while creating a safer, more streamlined environment for our teams. This facility supports how we operate today while positioning us for future growth."
COO Craig Faust · January 2026 facility announcement
What That Means in Practice
"Designing with intention" is not a construction phrase. It's an operations philosophy. Faust is saying Inpro wants to rethink how work gets done, not just where it gets done. That's a very different conversation than "we need more space." The manufacturers who use a facility build to redesign their operating systems come out with a structural advantage. The ones who move into new space running the same old processes just have a nicer building.
Where the Opportunity Is
Consolidating processes and modernizing workflows are Faust's words. Those are the exact problems that a connected manufacturing operations platform solves: order visibility, capacity planning, work order management, and service operations all running in one system instead of four. Building that in now, before the facility opens, means Inpro gets the modern operation it set out to build. Waiting until after occupancy means it gets the legacy operation in a new building.
Our Point of View

The Facility Is the Trigger. The Real Work Is What You Put Inside It.

A new manufacturing facility is a rare thing. It forces every process question. Where does this step happen? Who owns it? How does information flow from that station to the next? Most manufacturers answer those questions by recreating what they already do. The smart ones answer them by designing what they wish they'd had all along.

"You don't get a blank canvas very often. The manufacturers who use a build to redesign their operating model come out with a structural advantage over everyone who just moves their old processes into a new building."

Inpro makes architectural products that go into hospitals, universities, hotels, and commercial buildings all over the country. That means a mix of custom orders, standard catalog products, contractor timelines, and project-specific specs. Managing that complexity across a distributed team, multiple product lines, and a growing service and replacement parts business creates exactly the kind of operational overhead that a greenfield system design can eliminate.

The wall protection expansion specifically is a growth signal. More volume coming through the new facility means more work orders, more customer touchpoints, more scheduling complexity. The question isn't whether those processes need to be modernized. The question is whether you do it before the facility opens or after the first year of operation proves it's necessary.

🏗️
Greenfield decisions are cheaper than retrofit decisions. Always.

Building a connected operations system into a new facility before it opens costs a fraction of what it costs to implement after occupancy. The workflows aren't entrenched yet. The team isn't trained on the old way yet. The integrations are being defined for the first time. That window closes at move-in. Every month after occupancy that you run on legacy processes is a month that makes changing them harder and more expensive.

📋
"Consolidating processes" is the phrase that costs money when it's not done right.

Faust said the facility is designed to consolidate processes. Right now those processes probably live across multiple systems: an ERP, some spreadsheets, a CRM that doesn't talk to the shop floor, and an email thread that holds the institutional knowledge together. Consolidation without a connected platform just means the same processes in one building. Connected platform means one source of truth from order entry to shipment, visible to every team that needs it.

⚡
Scaling without adding headcount is only possible with the right system underneath.

The new facility expands capacity for the wall protection division. More capacity means more orders to manage. More orders means more coordination between sales, scheduling, and production. You can hire to absorb that coordination cost. Or you can build a system that handles the routine coordination automatically and lets your team focus on the exceptions. The first option scales linearly. The second doesn't scale at all. That's the difference Agentforce Operations makes.

🔗
Customer visibility and production visibility need to come from the same system.

When a contractor calls to ask where their order is, someone has to go find the answer. That answer might be in the ERP, might be on a production board, might be in someone's inbox. At low volume that's a minor annoyance. At the volume a new 30,000-square-foot facility is designed to produce, it becomes a customer service problem that erodes the relationships Inpro has spent decades building.
